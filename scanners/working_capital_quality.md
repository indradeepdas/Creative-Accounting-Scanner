# Working Capital Quality Scanner

## Purpose

Review receivables, inventory, payables, accruals, contract balances, and other current accounts to assess whether near-term operating performance is being supported or strained by working-capital timing.

## Core Principle

Working capital links operations, cash conversion, and future reversals. A quarterly result is more reliable when working-capital movements match the business explanation and do not simply borrow cash or profit from adjacent periods.

## When to Use

Use for full quarterly reports, cash-flow reviews, liquidity analysis, and any case where operating cash flow diverges from profit.

## Required Inputs

- Current and prior balance sheets.
- Cash-flow working-capital lines.
- Revenue, cost of goods sold, and operating expenses.
- Notes on receivables, inventory, payables, contract balances, and accrued expenses.

## Financial Statement Areas to Inspect

- Accounts receivable.
- Inventory.
- Prepaids and other current assets.
- Accounts payable.
- Accrued expenses.
- Deferred revenue and contract liabilities.
- Other current liabilities.

## Key Red-Flag Patterns

- DSO rises while revenue growth is emphasized.
- Inventory days rise while gross margin weakens.
- Payable days stretch materially, supporting operating cash flow.
- Accruals decline while related expenses rise.
- Deferred revenue falls while subscription revenue grows.
- Current assets or liabilities are reclassified in ways that reduce comparability.

## Suggested Calculations

- DSO.
- Inventory days.
- Days payable outstanding.
- Cash conversion cycle.
- Working capital as a percentage of revenue.
- Change in working capital as a percentage of operating cash flow.
- Contract liabilities as a percentage of revenue.

## Cross-Statement Triangulation

Compare working-capital movements with revenue, gross margin, cash flow, liquidity commentary, and demand indicators. Determine whether cash-flow strength comes from durable operations or one-time balance-sheet movement.

## Possible Benign Explanations

- Seasonality.
- Growth investment.
- Supplier term renegotiation.
- Inventory safety-stock build.
- Customer billing cycle changes.
- Acquisition integration.
- Supply-chain normalization.

## Escalation Logic

Escalate when working-capital movements materially explain earnings or cash-flow strength and are unlikely to repeat. Escalate further when the movement conflicts with management's operating narrative.

## Evidence Standard

Require period-over-period movement and related income-statement context. Avoid flagging normal seasonal working capital without prior-period comparison.

## Management Questions

- How much of operating cash flow came from working-capital movements?
- Are receivable, inventory, and payable days within normal seasonal ranges?
- What changed in customer and supplier terms?
- What working-capital movement is expected to reverse?
- Which balances are most sensitive to demand changes?

## Output Format

Provide a working-capital dashboard with DSO, inventory days, payable days, cash conversion cycle, cash-flow impact, benign explanations, and questions.

# Revenue Quality Scanner

## Purpose

Assess whether reported revenue appears supported by delivery, customer demand, collectibility, cash conversion, contract balances, and disclosures.

## Core Principle

Revenue quality is strongest when growth is repeatable, clearly explained, cash-backed, and consistent with customer-balance movements. Revenue deserves more scrutiny when it rises ahead of collections or depends on sensitive estimates.

## When to Use

Use for every quarterly report, earnings release, investor presentation, or segment update that includes revenue, billings, backlog, deferred revenue, contract assets, or customer commentary.

## Required Inputs

- Revenue by quarter and year-to-date period.
- Prior quarter and prior-year quarter revenue.
- Receivables, contract assets, deferred revenue, and customer advances.
- Revenue recognition policy and significant contract terms.
- Segment revenue where available.
- Cash from customers or operating cash-flow detail where available.

## Financial Statement Areas to Inspect

- Revenue and segment revenue.
- Accounts receivable and allowance accounts.
- Contract assets and unbilled revenue.
- Deferred revenue and contract liabilities.
- Sales returns, rebates, discounts, and variable consideration.
- Backlog, remaining performance obligations, and management commentary.

## Key Red-Flag Patterns

- Revenue growth materially exceeds cash collection or receivable conversion.
- Receivables, unbilled balances, or contract assets grow faster than revenue.
- Deferred revenue declines while revenue accelerates without a contract explanation.
- Large period-end sales, unusual shipment language, or customer acceptance uncertainty.
- Revenue depends on estimates of variable consideration, usage, completion, or collectibility.
- Revenue definition changes or gross-versus-net presentation changes without clear reconciliation.

## Suggested Calculations

- Revenue growth: current quarter versus prior-year quarter and prior quarter.
- Days sales outstanding: ending receivables divided by quarterly revenue times days in quarter.
- Receivables growth minus revenue growth.
- Contract assets as a percentage of revenue.
- Deferred revenue change as a percentage of revenue.
- Operating cash flow divided by revenue.
- Segment revenue growth versus consolidated revenue growth.

## Cross-Statement Triangulation

Compare revenue growth with receivables, contract assets, deferred revenue, operating cash flow, backlog, and segment commentary. If management cites demand strength, look for cash collection, order growth, pricing support, or volume evidence. If cash flow is weak, isolate whether working capital explains the difference.

## Possible Benign Explanations

- Seasonal billing cycles.
- Longer payment terms for enterprise or public-sector customers.
- A large contract milestone.
- Acquisition timing.
- Subscription mix shift.
- Price increases with delayed collection.
- New product launch or channel expansion.

## Escalation Logic

Escalate to Medium when one revenue-support metric deteriorates materially and disclosure is limited. Escalate to High when revenue growth, customer-balance expansion, cash-flow weakness, and weak disclosure appear together or recur across periods.

## Evidence Standard

Do not flag revenue quality from revenue growth alone. Require at least one supporting balance-sheet, cash-flow, contract-balance, or disclosure signal. Lower confidence when only an earnings release is available.

## Management Questions

- What drove the difference between revenue growth and cash collection?
- How much revenue came from new contracts, renewals, price, volume, and acquisitions?
- What explains changes in receivables, contract assets, and deferred revenue?
- Were any material sales subject to acceptance, return rights, extended payment terms, or concessions?
- How much revenue was recognized from prior-period contract liabilities?

## Output Format

Provide an evidence table with revenue trend, customer-balance trend, cash-flow support, interpretation, benign explanations, severity, evidence strength, confidence, and follow-up questions.

# Cash Flow Quality Scanner

## Purpose

Assess whether operating cash flow supports reported earnings after considering working-capital timing, nonrecurring cash items, classification, and capitalized recurring costs.

## Core Principle

Profit is higher quality when it converts to operating cash flow over time. Operating cash flow is also subject to timing and classification, so it should be adjusted for unusual cash movements before being used as confirmation.

## When to Use

Use for every full quarterly filing and whenever the user asks about earnings quality, cash conversion, adjusted EBITDA support, or free cash flow.

## Required Inputs

- Cash-flow statement.
- Income statement.
- Balance sheet.
- Working-capital detail.
- Nonrecurring cash items.
- Capital expenditures and capitalized recurring costs.
- Discontinued operations, tax, restructuring, and receivable sale disclosures where available.

## Financial Statement Areas to Inspect

- Operating cash flow.
- Net income and income from continuing operations.
- Depreciation, amortization, stock compensation, impairment, and deferred taxes.
- Receivables, inventory, payables, accrued expenses, and contract balances.
- Investing and financing cash-flow sections.
- Free cash flow and adjusted cash-flow metrics.

## Key Red-Flag Patterns

- Earnings grow faster than operating cash flow over several periods.
- Operating cash flow is positive mainly because of working-capital timing.
- Cash-flow classification changes improve operating cash flow.
- Receivable sales, factoring, or customer advances materially boost cash flow.
- Capitalized recurring costs sit outside operating cash flow.
- Adjusted EBITDA materially exceeds operating cash flow without explanation.

## Suggested Calculations

- Operating cash flow divided by net income.
- Operating cash flow divided by adjusted EBITDA.
- Adjusted operating cash flow excluding disclosed nonrecurring cash inflows or outflows.
- Free cash flow: operating cash flow minus capital expenditures and capitalized recurring costs where disclosed.
- Working-capital contribution to operating cash flow.
- Multi-quarter cash conversion average.

## Cross-Statement Triangulation

Bridge net income to operating cash flow. Identify whether the bridge is driven by noncash charges, working capital, or unusual cash items. Compare with receivables, inventory, payables, deferred revenue, and capitalized costs.

## Possible Benign Explanations

- Seasonal collection cycles.
- Temporary inventory build.
- Payment timing.
- Growth phase with upfront working capital.
- Subscription billings cycle.
- One-time restructuring cash payments.
- Tax timing.

## Escalation Logic

Escalate when weak cash conversion is repeated and explained by revenue-related balances or capitalized costs. Escalate further when operating cash flow appears improved by nonrecurring cash actions rather than operations.

## Evidence Standard

Use at least two periods where possible. A single quarter of weak cash flow is usually insufficient because quarterly cash flow is volatile.

## Management Questions

- What drove the difference between profit and operating cash flow?
- How much of operating cash flow came from working-capital timing?
- Were receivables sold, securitized, factored, or otherwise accelerated?
- What capitalized recurring costs should be considered in free cash flow?
- What cash-flow trend should users expect over the next two quarters?

## Output Format

Provide a profit-to-cash bridge with reported profit, operating cash flow, working-capital drivers, unusual cash items, adjusted cash conversion, and confidence.

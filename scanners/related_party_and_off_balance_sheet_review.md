# Related Party And Off Balance Sheet Review Scanner

## Purpose

Review disclosed related-party transactions, unconsolidated entities, guarantees, purchase commitments, receivable sales, leases, special financing arrangements, and other arrangements that may affect earnings quality or risk outside headline metrics.

## Core Principle

Some economically important obligations, dependencies, or transactions are disclosed outside primary statement line items. Earnings quality analysis should include these arrangements because they can affect revenue, cash flow, leverage, liquidity, and comparability.

## When to Use

Use when related-party notes, variable interest entities, guarantees, lease obligations, supplier financing, factoring, securitization, commitments, or unusual counterparty transactions are disclosed.

## Required Inputs

- Related-party notes.
- Commitments and contingencies.
- Debt, lease, and guarantee disclosures.
- Cash-flow statement.
- Revenue and receivable notes.
- Segment or customer concentration disclosures.
- Management discussion of liquidity and financing.

## Financial Statement Areas to Inspect

- Related-party transactions and balances.
- Unconsolidated entities and variable interests.
- Guarantees and commitments.
- Leases and purchase obligations.
- Receivable sales, factoring, and securitization.
- Supplier financing or reverse factoring.
- Customer concentration and channel arrangements.

## Key Red-Flag Patterns

- Material transactions with related parties lack business-purpose explanation.
- Revenue or profit depends on counterparties with unclear economics.
- Receivables are sold or financed, boosting operating cash flow.
- Guarantees or commitments are large relative to liquidity.
- Off-balance-sheet obligations grow while leverage metrics look stable.
- Supplier financing improves working capital without clear disclosure of terms.

## Suggested Calculations

- Related-party revenue or expense as a percentage of total revenue or expense.
- Related-party receivables as a percentage of total receivables.
- Guarantees and commitments as a percentage of equity and operating cash flow.
- Receivables sold as a percentage of operating cash flow.
- Lease and purchase obligations by maturity.
- Supplier-finance balances as a percentage of payables.

## Cross-Statement Triangulation

Compare disclosed arrangements with revenue quality, customer balances, operating cash flow, leverage, and liquidity. Determine whether the arrangement changes the interpretation of reported performance or risk.

## Possible Benign Explanations

- Normal joint venture or equity-method investment activity.
- Industry-standard supplier financing.
- Efficient receivable monetization with transparent terms.
- Strategic partnership with clear commercial rationale.
- Guarantees required for ordinary-course operations.

## Escalation Logic

Escalate when arrangements are material, complex, poorly explained, or connected to improved revenue, cash flow, or leverage metrics. Escalate further when related-party economics are unclear or obligations are large relative to liquidity.

## Evidence Standard

Use only disclosed arrangements. Do not infer undisclosed relationships. Flag missing explanation, materiality, complexity, and metric impact rather than intent.

## Management Questions

- What is the commercial purpose of the arrangement?
- What amounts are recognized in revenue, expenses, assets, liabilities, and cash flow?
- What obligations remain after quarter-end?
- How would leverage, working capital, or operating cash flow look without the arrangement?
- What governance process reviews related-party terms?

## Output Format

Provide an arrangement table with counterparty type, statement impact, cash-flow impact, obligations, disclosure quality, benign explanations, and follow-up questions.

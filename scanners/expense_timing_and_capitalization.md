# Expense Timing And Capitalization Scanner

## Purpose

Assess whether costs are recognized in the current period or deferred into assets in a way that materially affects margins, operating income, or future expense burden.

## Core Principle

Capitalization can be appropriate when costs create future benefits, but earnings quality weakens when capitalization rises faster than the related activity, amortization periods stretch, or expenses are deferred without clear support.

## When to Use

Use when margins improve unexpectedly, capitalized costs are material, software development or contract acquisition costs are disclosed, amortization expense changes, or cash outflows exceed recognized expense.

## Required Inputs

- Operating expense detail.
- Capitalized cost additions.
- Amortization expense.
- Property, plant, equipment, intangibles, contract cost assets, and software assets.
- Cash-flow statement.
- Accounting policies for capitalization and amortization.

## Financial Statement Areas to Inspect

- Cost of revenue and operating expenses.
- Capitalized software, contract acquisition costs, and internal-use assets.
- Property, plant, equipment, and intangibles.
- Amortization and depreciation.
- Cash paid for capital expenditures and capitalized costs.
- Impairment and restructuring notes.

## Key Red-Flag Patterns

- Capitalized costs rise while related expense ratios fall.
- Amortization expense declines or grows slowly despite rising capitalized balances.
- Useful lives or amortization periods are extended with limited explanation.
- Operating cash flow is adjusted by adding back costs that are recurring cash outflows.
- Margin expansion depends on moving costs from expense to assets.
- Impairment charges follow a period of aggressive asset growth.

## Suggested Calculations

- Capitalized costs as a percentage of revenue.
- Capitalized costs as a percentage of related cash spend.
- Amortization expense divided by average capitalized cost balance.
- Implied amortization period: average capitalized balance divided by amortization expense.
- Operating margin before and after capitalized cost additions.
- Free cash flow after capitalized costs.

## Cross-Statement Triangulation

Compare margin improvement with capitalized cost additions, amortization, cash-flow investing outflows, and policy disclosures. Check whether asset growth aligns with revenue growth, product launches, contract wins, or capacity expansion.

## Possible Benign Explanations

- Investment phase before a product launch.
- Contract assets tied to multi-year revenue.
- New systems implementation.
- Longer-lived assets supported by operational data.
- Acquisition-related asset recognition.
- Temporary spend cycle.

## Escalation Logic

Escalate when capitalization increases materially and the company provides little detail on future benefits. Escalate to High when capitalized balances, slowing amortization, weak cash flow, and later impairments appear together.

## Evidence Standard

Require a link between capitalization and reported performance. Do not flag capitalization merely because it exists; show how it affects margins, operating income, cash flow, or future expense burden.

## Management Questions

- What types of costs were capitalized this quarter?
- What future benefits support capitalization?
- How are useful lives determined and reviewed?
- What would operating income look like if capitalization had matched prior-period rates?
- What impairment indicators were assessed?

## Output Format

Provide a cost recognition table showing reported expense, capitalized additions, amortization, cash outflow, margin effect, and follow-up questions.

# Asset Quality Scanner

## Purpose

Review whether reported assets appear recoverable, current, and supported by future cash flows or operating use.

## Core Principle

Assets store costs and expected benefits. Earnings quality weakens when assets grow faster than the business they support, when write-downs appear delayed, or when valuation allowances and reserves do not reflect risk.

## When to Use

Use when inventory, receivables, contract assets, deferred tax assets, investments, goodwill, intangibles, or other assets are material or growing faster than revenue.

## Required Inputs

- Balance sheet for current and prior periods.
- Revenue, cost of goods sold, and cash-flow data.
- Inventory, impairment, fair value, tax, and credit-risk notes.
- Segment performance where available.
- Acquisition and divestiture disclosures.

## Financial Statement Areas to Inspect

- Receivables and contract assets.
- Inventory.
- Property, plant, and equipment.
- Goodwill and intangible assets.
- Deferred tax assets.
- Investments and fair value measurements.
- Other assets and prepaid balances.

## Key Red-Flag Patterns

- Assets grow faster than revenue, gross profit, or cash flow.
- Inventory days increase while demand or margins weaken.
- Goodwill or intangibles remain stable despite segment underperformance.
- Deferred tax assets increase while profitability weakens.
- Investment valuations rely on unobservable inputs with limited sensitivity disclosure.
- Large "other assets" balances lack detail.

## Suggested Calculations

- Asset growth versus revenue growth.
- Inventory days: ending inventory divided by quarterly cost of goods sold times days in quarter.
- Gross margin trend versus inventory growth.
- Goodwill and intangibles as a percentage of equity and assets.
- Deferred tax assets as a percentage of pre-tax income.
- Return on assets by period.

## Cross-Statement Triangulation

Compare asset growth with revenue, margin, cash flow, segment performance, impairment indicators, and management commentary. If assets rise while cash flow and margins weaken, ask whether recoverability support is visible.

## Possible Benign Explanations

- Inventory build for confirmed orders.
- Capacity expansion.
- Acquisition accounting.
- Temporary supply-chain strategy.
- Tax attributes supported by credible future income.
- Long-lived assets in early ramp-up.

## Escalation Logic

Escalate when asset growth is material, unsupported by operating metrics, and poorly explained. Escalate further when a later impairment, write-down, or valuation allowance appears likely based on disclosed performance.

## Evidence Standard

Require at least one performance or recoverability indicator beyond balance-sheet size. Use caution when the company is acquisitive or seasonal.

## Management Questions

- What operating evidence supports recoverability of the asset balance?
- How much asset growth came from acquisitions versus organic activity?
- What changed in inventory aging or obsolescence risk?
- What impairment indicators were considered?
- What sensitivity exists around fair value or tax valuation assumptions?

## Output Format

Provide an asset-quality matrix by asset class with trend, related income-statement metric, cash-flow support, disclosure quality, benign explanations, and escalation status.

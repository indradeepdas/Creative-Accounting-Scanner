# Disclosure Change Detection Scanner

## Purpose

Identify changes in disclosure wording, metric definitions, segment structure, accounting policies, risk language, and table availability that may affect comparability or signal a need for follow-up.

## Core Principle

Disclosures are part of the evidence. A change in what management explains, omits, emphasizes, or redefines can be as important as a numerical movement.

## When to Use

Use when prior-period documents are available, when metrics change, when segment definitions shift, or when a sensitive account grows while disclosure detail declines.

## Required Inputs

- Current and prior quarterly filings.
- Current and prior earnings releases.
- Investor presentations.
- Prior annual report if available.
- Metric definitions and reconciliations.

## Financial Statement Areas to Inspect

- Accounting policies.
- Critical estimates.
- Revenue and customer-balance notes.
- Segment disclosures.
- Risk factors and liquidity discussion.
- Non-GAAP definitions.
- Tables for backlog, remaining performance obligations, and working capital.

## Key Red-Flag Patterns

- A metric definition changes without a bridge.
- A segment is reorganized after underperformance.
- A sensitive table disappears or becomes less granular.
- Risk language worsens while the related accounting estimate is unchanged.
- Management commentary changes tone without numerical explanation.
- Prior-period comparatives are omitted when trend analysis would be useful.

## Suggested Calculations

- Count and compare reported metrics across periods.
- Reconcile old and new segment measures where possible.
- Measure affected balances as a percentage of revenue, assets, or income.
- Compare disclosure length and table granularity for sensitive areas.
- Calculate trend under both old and new definitions when data permits.

## Cross-Statement Triangulation

Tie disclosure changes to the underlying financial movement. A definition change matters most when it changes trend interpretation or obscures a deteriorating balance, margin, or cash-flow measure.

## Possible Benign Explanations

- New accounting standard.
- Business reorganization.
- Acquisition or divestiture.
- Improved disclosure design.
- Immateriality.
- Regulator or exchange guidance.

## Escalation Logic

Escalate when a disclosure change materially reduces comparability in an area with unfavorable trends. Escalate to High when multiple sensitive disclosures change at once and reconciliations are missing.

## Evidence Standard

Require a current-versus-prior comparison. Do not flag a disclosure as missing unless a prior document or standard user expectation supports the observation.

## Management Questions

- Why did the definition, segment, or table change?
- Can management provide a bridge to the prior presentation?
- What trend would appear under the old definition?
- Why was prior-period detail reduced?
- Which financial statement line items are affected?

## Output Format

Provide a disclosure-change log with old disclosure, new disclosure, affected metric or account, comparability effect, benign explanation, and question.

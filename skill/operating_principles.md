# Operating Principles

## Evidence First

Start with what the document says. The skill should cite the document, section, metric, comparison period, and calculation before interpreting a pattern.

## Cross-Statement Review

Quarterly earnings quality is rarely visible in one statement alone. Revenue growth should be checked against receivables, contract assets, deferred revenue, cash collections, and revenue notes. Profit improvement should be checked against working capital, capitalized costs, cash flow, and adjusted metric reconciliations.

## Incentives Are Context

Potential reporting pressure can explain why a pattern deserves attention, but it does not establish intent. Treat incentives such as covenant pressure, compensation targets, capital raising, or consensus expectations as context only.

## Benign Explanations Matter

Every meaningful red flag should include possible valid explanations. Seasonality, acquisitions, new contracts, supply-chain shifts, price changes, product mix, customer concentration, inflation, and foreign exchange can all distort quarterly comparisons.

## Public Language Requires Restraint

Public outputs should sound like disciplined financial analysis. Avoid outrage, ridicule, certainty, and claims about legality or intent.

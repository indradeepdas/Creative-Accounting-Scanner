# Classification And Presentation

## What The Principle Means

Classification and presentation affect how users perceive recurring performance. The same net income can tell a different story depending on whether an item is shown in revenue, cost of goods sold, operating expense, other income, discontinued operations, adjusted EBITDA, or a footnote.

## Why It Matters In Quarterly Reporting

Quarterly readers often focus on headline revenue, gross margin, operating income, adjusted metrics, and management commentary. Classification can therefore influence the perceived trend even when total profit is unchanged.

## How The Issue Can Affect Reported Performance

Revenue may be shown gross rather than net. Costs may move between cost of goods sold and operating expense. Gains may be included in operating income. Recurring costs may be labeled unusual. Discontinued or restructuring items may be presented in ways that make continuing performance look cleaner.

## Where It May Appear In The Financial Statements

- Revenue presentation.
- Gross margin and cost classification.
- Selling, general, and administrative expense.
- Operating income.
- Other income and expense.
- Discontinued operations.
- Restructuring, impairment, and special charges.
- Non-GAAP or adjusted measures.

## How To Triangulate It Across Statements

Compare statement classification with notes, reconciliations, and commentary. Rebuild a simple recurring operating profit view when possible: revenue less normal cost of revenue, normal operating expenses, and recurring cash costs. Check whether classification changes explain trend shifts.

## Typical Red-Flag Patterns

- Gross margin improves because costs are moved below the gross-profit line.
- Operating income includes gains or excludes recurring operating costs.
- Revenue switches from net to gross presentation without enough explanation.
- "One-time" charges recur over many quarters.
- Adjusted metrics remove normal cash operating costs.
- Labels change while underlying economic activity appears similar.

## Possible Benign Explanations

- Business model changed from agent to principal or the reverse.
- New accounting guidance affected presentation.
- Segment realignment changed cost allocation.
- A real divestiture or exit created discontinued operations.
- Management added supplemental metrics to improve transparency.

## Questions A Reviewer Should Ask

- Did any line-item definition change?
- Would margin trends look different under the prior classification?
- Are adjusted metrics reconciled clearly?
- Are excluded costs genuinely unusual?
- Does management explain how presentation changed comparability?

## Limitations Of The Analysis

Public filings may not provide enough detail to fully recast the statements. Some classification choices are permitted and require industry context.

## How The Codex Skill Should Use The Principle

Use this principle when scanner results depend on line-item labels. The skill should look past labels, describe the underlying economic item, and test whether trend interpretation changes under a normalized presentation.

# Accruals Cash And Timing

## What The Principle Means

Accrual accounting recognizes revenue and expenses when earned or incurred, not necessarily when cash moves. This creates timing differences that are normal but analytically important. Earnings quality weakens when accrual growth repeatedly lacks cash support or when timing benefits reverse later.

## Why It Matters In Quarterly Reporting

Quarterly results are especially sensitive to cutoff, billings, collections, inventory receipts, supplier payments, and accrual true-ups. A single large shipment, customer payment, prepayment, or accrual adjustment can change the quarter's story.

## How The Issue Can Affect Reported Performance

Revenue can be recognized before collection. Expenses can be accrued, deferred, capitalized, or released. Operating cash flow can be helped by collecting receivables, stretching payables, reducing inventory, securitizing balances, or receiving customer advances. These actions may be normal, but they can make short-period performance look stronger or weaker than the underlying run rate.

## Where It May Appear In The Financial Statements

- Accounts receivable and contract assets.
- Deferred revenue and contract liabilities.
- Inventory and cost of goods sold.
- Prepaid expenses and capitalized costs.
- Accrued expenses, provisions, and other current liabilities.
- Operating cash-flow working-capital lines.
- Noncash and nonrecurring cash-flow adjustments.

## How To Triangulate It Across Statements

Bridge profit to operating cash flow. Identify whether the difference comes from receivables, inventory, payables, contract balances, accruals, or noncash charges. Then compare those movements with revenue, gross margin, and notes. A cash shortfall is more concerning when it aligns with fast-growing customer balances and limited disclosure.

## Typical Red-Flag Patterns

- Earnings rise while operating cash flow falls for several periods.
- Receivables or contract assets grow faster than revenue.
- Inventory rises faster than cost of goods sold or sales.
- Payables stretch materially without liquidity explanation.
- Customer advances decline while revenue grows.
- Large working-capital improvement is described as operational but appears one-off.

## Possible Benign Explanations

- Seasonal billing and collection cycles.
- Growth in enterprise customers with longer payment terms.
- Inventory build for a launch or supply constraint.
- Planned payment timing.
- New contract structures.
- Tax timing.
- Acquisition integration.

## Questions A Reviewer Should Ask

- Which working-capital accounts explain the profit-to-cash gap?
- Are the changes seasonal or unusual?
- Did collections after period-end support the receivable balance?
- Are customer advances consistent with revenue recognition?
- Did operating cash flow benefit from one-time actions?

## Limitations Of The Analysis

Operating cash flow is itself affected by timing and classification. A weak quarter of cash flow can be benign, and a strong quarter can be temporary. Multi-period analysis is essential.

## How The Codex Skill Should Use The Principle

Use this principle in every full review. The skill should compute profit-to-cash bridges, days metrics where possible, working-capital movements as a percentage of revenue, and adjusted operating cash flow when unusual cash items are disclosed.

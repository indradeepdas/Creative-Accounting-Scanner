# Sustainable Versus Transitory Performance

## What The Principle Means

Sustainable performance is the portion of earnings and cash flow likely to recur under normal business conditions. Transitory performance comes from one-time gains, unusual losses, reserve true-ups, discrete tax items, restructuring, litigation, divestitures, acquisitions, or temporary working-capital actions.

## Why It Matters In Quarterly Reporting

A single quarter can be dominated by items that are not representative. Users often extrapolate quarterly results, so identifying transitory effects is essential before judging momentum.

## How The Issue Can Affect Reported Performance

Transitory gains can inflate revenue, margin, operating income, net income, adjusted EBITDA, or operating cash flow. Transitory charges can depress GAAP measures while adjusted measures look strong. Either direction can distort the reader's view of run-rate profitability.

## Where It May Appear In The Financial Statements

- Other income and expense.
- Restructuring and impairment charges.
- Litigation settlements.
- Gains on asset sales.
- Discontinued operations.
- Tax provision.
- Working-capital changes.
- Adjusted metric reconciliations.

## How To Triangulate It Across Statements

Identify nonrecurring income-statement items and their cash effects. Adjust both income and operating cash flow consistently where possible. Compare the adjusted trend with unadjusted results and management's own reconciliation.

## Typical Red-Flag Patterns

- Adjusted metrics remove recurring costs but retain recurring benefits.
- Large gains are included in operating performance.
- Recurring restructuring or integration charges are excluded every quarter.
- Operating cash flow benefits from a one-time receivable sale or inventory action.
- Tax benefits drive EPS improvement without operating support.

## Possible Benign Explanations

- A genuine asset sale or legal settlement occurred.
- Restructuring is part of a documented plan with clear milestones.
- Acquisition integration costs are time-limited.
- Tax items are discrete and properly disclosed.
- Working-capital actions reflect durable process improvements.

## Questions A Reviewer Should Ask

- Which items should not be annualized?
- Are exclusions symmetrical, or do they remove only unfavorable items?
- Do cash-flow adjustments match income adjustments?
- How often has the company reported similar "one-time" items?
- What evidence shows the item will not recur?

## Limitations Of The Analysis

External users cannot always determine recurrence from one filing. Some items recur for valid reasons in acquisitive or restructuring-heavy companies.

## How The Codex Skill Should Use The Principle

Use this principle to normalize output. The skill should present reported results, adjusted management results, and reviewer-normalized observations separately, explaining the reason for any difference.
